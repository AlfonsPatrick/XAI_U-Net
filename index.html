Methodology

How Integrated Gradients explains the model's predictions

1. Interpolation Path

Create a series of interpolated images between a baseline (black image) and the input CT scan, parameterized by α ∈ [0, 1].

xα = x' + α · (x − x')

2. Gradient Computation

At each interpolation step, compute the gradient of the model's output with respect to the input image.

∂F(xα) / ∂xi

3. Path Integration

Approximate the integral of these gradients using a Riemann sum (average of gradients across all steps).

IGi(x) = (xi − x'i) × ∫01 ∂F(xα)/∂xi dα

4. Attribution Map

The resulting map shows each pixel's contribution to the prediction. Red = positive attribution, Blue = negative attribution.

Why Integrated Gradients?

Unlike traditional visualization methods like Grad-CAM that suffer from poor resolution and gradient saturation, Integrated Gradients provides pixel-perfect attribution. By integrating gradients along a path from a baseline, it guarantees that any feature contributing to the model's output receives an exact importance score, making it highly robust for medical imaging where tiny details matter.

Sensitivity If a feature changes the prediction, it gets non-zero attribution. This ensures no crucial finding is ignored by the explainer.
Implementation Invariance Two functionally equivalent models always give identical attributions, providing a highly reliable and consistent explanation.
Completeness Attributions sum perfectly to the difference between the input and baseline predictions, ensuring mathematical accountability.

Model Performance & Results

Training history and sample predictions across validation data

Sample Predictions

This sample presents predictions across all three categories — Normal, Malignant, and Benign — giving a reasonably complete picture of the model's behavior.

Normal cases (columns 1, 3, 6) are handled well by the model. The original CT slices in these cases show varying anatomical levels, with some appearing more abdominal in nature rather than classic lung window views. All three ground truth masks are empty, correctly reflecting the absence of any nodule. The model's predicted masks are also completely black across all Normal cases, indicating the model produces no false positives in this category. This suggests good specificity and that the model is not prone to hallucinating nodules where none exist.

Malignant cases (columns 2 and 4) demonstrate the model's strongest performance. In column 4, a large and irregularly shaped malignant nodule is clearly annotated in the ground truth, and the predicted mask reproduces its shape, size, and location with high accuracy. Column 2 presents a smaller malignant nodule, which the model still successfully detects, though the predicted region appears marginally smaller than the ground truth annotation. Overall, the model shows reliable sensitivity and spatial accuracy for malignant nodule segmentation.

Benign cases (column 5) reveal a clear weakness. The ground truth mask shows two very small, faint nodule specks — characteristic of benign lesions which tend to be smaller and lower in contrast. The predicted mask shows only partial detection at best, with faint dots that may or may not correspond precisely to the ground truth locations. This suggests the model struggles with benign nodules, likely due to their subtle appearance and potentially insufficient representation during training.

Overall, the model performs well on Normal and Malignant categories but shows inconsistency on Benign cases. Improving benign detection should be the primary focus going forward, as missing small benign nodules carries real clinical risk.

Training History

The training history spans approximately 160 epochs with the following observations across key metrics.

Training Dice Coefficient & Loss show a steep and healthy improvement in the early phase. The dice coefficient jumped from near zero (~0.004) at epoch 1 to around 0.60 by epoch 30, with the dice loss dropping correspondingly from ~0.996 to ~0.40. This indicates the model learned the core segmentation task quickly in the first phase. Beyond epoch 30, progress continued but at a slower pace, eventually plateauing in the 0.73–0.76 range with noticeable oscillation in later epochs, suggesting the model had largely converged but was still fluctuating rather than cleanly settling.

IoU followed the same trend — starting near zero and climbing to roughly 0.57–0.60 in later epochs — consistent with the dice score behavior, confirming that spatial overlap quality improved steadily before leveling off.

Validation Recall shows an interesting anomaly: it opened at 0.78 in epoch 1, then sharply crashed to 0.065 in epoch 2 before gradually recovering. This initial spike is likely a coincidental result from random weight initialization rather than true learning. After recovering, recall climbed steadily and plateaued around 0.67–0.68 in the final epochs, meaning the model correctly detects roughly two-thirds of true positive nodule regions.

Learning Rate Schedule reveals an aggressive halving strategy — starting at 5×10⁻⁴ and repeatedly halving down to a floor of 1×10⁻⁷ by the final epochs. While this helped stabilize training, the learning rate became effectively negligible in the last 20–30 epochs, meaning the model stopped meaningfully learning. This aggressive decay likely locked the model into a local optimum before it could fully resolve the benign class detection weakness.

Overall, training was stable and progressed well, but the combination of an overly aggressive learning rate decay and class imbalance appears to have caused the model to converge prematurely to a malignant-biased solution.

Model Architecture

Attention U-Net with Focal Tversky Loss

Attention U-Net Structure

U-Net is a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) architecture specifically designed for biomedical image segmentation. Its distinctive U-shape consists of a contracting path to capture context, and a symmetric expansive path to enable precise localization.

Attention U-Net integrates Attention Gates (AGs) into the skip connections. These gates allow the model to automatically learn to focus on target structures of varying shapes and sizes, while suppressing feature responses in irrelevant regions, ensuring the model implicitly learns where to "look" without requiring external organ localization modules.

Training Configuration

  • Loss FunctionFocal Tversky (α=0.4, β=0.6)
  • OptimizerAdam (lr=5×10⁻⁴)
  • Epochs160 (early stopping)
  • Batch Size8
  • Dropout0.5
  • RegularizationL2 (1×10⁻⁵)
  • Threshold0.4 (segmentation)
  • Post-processingRemove small objects (<100px)
Focal Tversky Loss is specifically designed to address severe class imbalance in medical image segmentation. By adjusting α and β, it prioritizes the minority positive class (nodules) over the overwhelming background, forcing the model to focus on hard-to-detect lesions.

Study Cases

Analysis of model limitations and anomalies discovered using Integrated Gradients

Study Case 1

This is a critical case study that highlights the irreplaceable role of XAI. Metrically, the model's performance on this case is very impressive. The resulting segmentation is highly accurate and nearly identical to the ground truth, achieving a Dice Score of 0.91. Based on this number alone, the prediction would be considered an absolute success.

However, investigation using IG reveals a concerning reasoning anomaly. The attribution map shows that the highest positive importance score is not centered on the nodule mass, but rather on adjacent structures. This finding proves that the model has learned a shortcut and relies on spurious correlations to produce the correct prediction for the wrong reason.

Anomalies like this are impossible to detect with quantitative metrics and are clear evidence of the danger of relying solely on numbers for medical model validation. It shows that the model might be unreliable in similar cases where the nodule is not adjacent to other structures such as blood vessels.

Study Case 2

XAI analysis is also important to validate the model's specificity, i.e., its ability to ignore irrelevant areas. This case displays an area of healthy lung tissue without any nodules.

As seen in the images, the model correctly did not produce segmentation in that area. The generated attribution map is also completely empty. This transparently confirms that the model does not hallucinate or find spurious features when there are no relevant targets. It is strong evidence that the model specifically looks for features associated with nodules and remains silent if those features are absent.

Study Case 3

This final case tests how XAI can provide transparency into the model's limitations and failures. In this case, there is a small nodule present in the ground truth, but the model failed to detect it. The model did not produce a segmentation mask for the nodule, only masks following the body and lung boundaries.

Analysis using Integrated Gradients (IG) provides deep insights into this failure. The generated attribution map shows that the model did indeed score some pixels as important features, but in completely incorrect locations. The positive attribution produced by the model does not correlate with the actual nodule location, but rather resides on another lesion.

This finding is crucial because it shows the model is easily distracted by ambiguous features like random noise and contrasting anatomical boundaries. It can be concluded that in this case, the model didn't just fail, but made the wrong decision for the wrong reason. This clearly indicates the model is less reliable in detecting nodules adjacent to the lung wall, a specific weakness that would remain hidden without XAI analysis.
